# GABAergic Control and Disinhibition in Entorhinal-Hippocampal Circuits

The coordination between the medial entorhinal cortex (MEC) and the hippocampal formation relies on complex inhibitory circuit motifs, where GABAergic interneurons act as dynamic gatekeepers of information flow. Disinhibition—a process in which GABAergic interneurons are themselves inhibited—serves as a crucial computational mechanism to modulate hippocampal responsiveness to extrinsic cortical input. This interaction is essential for regulating pattern separation, cognitive map formation, and the temporal gating of memory-relevant activity within hippocampal subfields.

In both the hippocampus and the MEC, GABAergic interneurons—particularly parvalbumin-positive (PV+) populations—are central to maintaining the sparse firing patterns necessary for neural computation. Research into microcircuit motifs indicates that feedback inhibition, often involving PV+ cells, exerts a divisive effect on pyramidal cells, helping to disentangle superimposed firing patterns and promote a sparse assembly code [1705.07614]. When these inhibitory neurons are inhibited by other interneuron subtypes, the resulting disinhibition allows the hippocampus to toggle between states of signal integration and signal filtering, a mechanism implicated in the modulation of contextual memory formation [1711.09876].

The functional impact of this inhibitory architecture is most visible in the dentate gyrus (DG), where the interplay between adult-born immature granule cells (imGCs) and mature granule cells (mGCs) exemplifies the importance of inhibitory control. While mGCs rely on strong feedback inhibition to remain sparse and thus highly effective at pattern separation, highly excitable imGCs demonstrate how shifts in the local inhibitory balance can transition a circuit from pattern separation to pattern integration [2301.05387]. This heterogeneity suggests that the entorhinal-hippocampal system uses precise control over GABAergic tone to dynamically allocate computational resources according to task demands.

Furthermore, the temporal dynamics of these cell assemblies exhibit a core-periphery structure, where inhibitory cells often occupy central roles in the functional connectivity network [2001.06371]. The ability of these circuits to maintain stable representations—such as those required for grid cells in the MEC and place cells in the hippocampus—depends on the precise modulation of inhibitory inputs to ensure that navigation-related information is accurately broadcasted and processed without excessive noise [1712.04602]. By using mechanisms analogous to locality-sensitive hashing to generate sparse codes, the hippocampus effectively leverages these interneuronal motifs to process high-dimensional spatial data [1910.04590].

Computational models exploring these circuits highlight that disinhibitory pathways from the cortex contribute significantly to the ability to navigate complex environments and form stable memory associations. By providing "context-sensitive bias," these pathways enable the system to balance memory formation with ongoing network activity, effectively scaling the network's computational capacity without requiring physical expansion [1711.09876]. This level of control is fundamental to the compositional nature of cognitive maps, where grid-like activity from the MEC is bound to hippocampal place cell representations to allow for flexible, hierarchical spatial reasoning [2406.18808].

Ultimately, the disinhibition of GABAergic interneurons provides a high-fidelity control mechanism that allows the medial entorhinal-hippocampal system to switch between specialized computational states. This regulatory architecture is essential for balancing the competing needs of pattern separation and information integration, ensuring that spatial and contextual memories are prioritized and protected from interference.
